# Use Modalities and Rubrics

## Modalities

The modality governs the overall shape of a run. It is not just a description of what the user asked for — it is a decision about what kind of work the inquiry actually requires. The same surface question can require different modalities depending on what is blocking or opening the inquiry.

### Diagnostic

Use when the aim is to surface hidden structure, contradiction, blockage, repression, or frame failure.

Diagnostic mode is called for when something is not working and it is not obvious why, when a contradiction keeps returning no matter how it is addressed, when the explicit account of a field or situation is clearly missing something, or when the inquiry is stuck because it is asking the wrong question.

The primary tool of diagnostic work is CMEA — comparing what something says it is doing against what it actually depends on. The topology is often `klein`, because diagnosis requires holding inside and outside together.

A diagnostic run does not begin with recommendations. It begins with looking.

### Applicative

Use when the aim is to move from a current state to a better state through action and verification.

Applicative mode is called for when the problem is clear enough to act on, when implementation is the live issue, when an experiment needs to be run, or when state change with verifiable outcome is the goal.

The primary tool of applicative work is the `L4'` loop: Observe, Think, Plan, Build, Execute, Verify, Learn. The topology is often `torus`, though it may need to escalate if contradictions emerge during the run.

An applicative run builds its evaluative apparatus before it acts. Executing without success criteria is not applicative work — it is motion without verification.

### Explorative

Use when the aim is to open the field, discover what the inquiry actually is, and avoid premature closure.

Explorative mode is called for when the real question is not yet clear, when the field has not been shaped enough for diagnosis or application, when the task is to discover what needs to be discovered rather than to apply what is already known.

Explorative work is QL-heavy: question-shaping and positional mapping. When those reveal a binary wall — a contradiction the field cannot metabolize — tetralemma is deployed from MEF's L2 to open the logical space. QL identifies the shape of the inquiry; MEF supplies the rubric for traversing it when logic breaks down. The topology is often `torus` to start, escalating when the field shows its shape.

Explorative mode should reclassify quickly once the real pressure becomes visible. Prolonged exploration without reclassification is avoidance, not method.

### Explanatory

Use when the aim is to make a structure, case, or relation intelligible — to teach, clarify, compare, or walk someone through what is already in view.

Explanatory mode is called for when the primary task is communication rather than discovery or action. It uses the same resources and rubrics as other modes but exposes them more explicitly.

The failure mode of explanatory work is flattening — reducing the complexity of the system to slogans in order to make it accessible. Accessibility and fidelity are both required. When they conflict, the right move is to acknowledge the tension rather than sacrifice one silently.

## Braiding Modalities

Runs may braid modalities, but they still need a primary mode governing the work and at most one secondary. Operating multiple modalities at equal weight usually means the inquiry has not yet been shaped.

The most common braid: `explorative` to find the real question, followed by `diagnostic` or `applicative` once the question is clear.

## Core Rubrics

### Tetralemma

The primary rubric for coherent paradox. Works five positions: IS, IS-NOT, BOTH, NEITHER, SILENCE.

Tetralemma is an MEF rubric — specifically the instrument of L2, the Logical lens in Square C. It is not a QL tool. QL structures the inquiry and maps its positions; when that inquiry hits a binary wall that cannot be resolved by better framing, MEF's L2 supplies tetralemma to open the logical space. The distinction matters: QL and tetralemma are not the same operation, and conflating them collapses the difference between structural inquiry (QL) and logical critique of how a field handles its own contradictions (MEF L2).

Use tetralemma when a problem is stuck in binary framing, when affirmation and negation are both required, when the question itself may be malformed, or when the honest limit of articulation needs to be named rather than plastered over.

The discipline is to run all five positions in sequence rather than jumping to the more sophisticated-sounding ones. `BOTH` as compromise language and `SILENCE` as theatrical mystery are both failures. The moves have to be grounded in the actual inquiry.

### CMEA (Critical Meta-Epistemic Analysis)

The primary diagnostic rubric for MEF work. Compares explicit and implicit structure, traces privilege-repression dynamics, identifies anomalies and paradigm incoherence.

Use CMEA when what a field, discourse, design, or system says it is doing does not match what it actually depends on. The six-lens sweep is the instrument. Full sweep for serious diagnosis; focused two-to-four lens pass when the pressure is more local.

CMEA is not a way of looking clever. It is a way of finding what is actually wrong — which means finding what the field cannot see about itself.

### L4' Scientific Loop

The primary applicative rubric. Structures movement from current state to ideal state through: Observe, Think, Plan, Build, Execute, Verify, and outer Learn.

Build comes before Execute — always. Building the evaluative apparatus before acting is not optional. Without it, verification is theater and the loop learns nothing.

## Rubric and Modality Alignment

The rubrics have natural modality affinities but can be used across modes:

- Tetralemma is most at home in `diagnostic` and `explorative` work, but is used wherever a binary is distorting the inquiry.
- CMEA is most at home in `diagnostic` work, but the lens structure can inform explanation and exploration too.
- The `L4'` loop is most at home in `applicative` work, but its Observe and Think phases are useful in explorative runs.

Choosing the right modality and the right rubric together is part of what `using-epi-logos` determines before anything else begins.
